Domain-Generalized Multimodal GeoAI for Climate-Smart Agriculture

My current research focuses on building trustworthy multimodal geospatial AI systems for climate-smart agriculture and environmental resilience. I am particularly interested in how satellite-based AI models can generalize beyond the regions, years, crop systems, and climate conditions on which they were trained.

Modern agricultural monitoring increasingly relies on Earth-observation data such as Sentinel-1 SAR, Sentinel-2 optical imagery, weather variables, terrain, and environmental covariates. These data sources provide complementary information about crop growth, vegetation condition, moisture, structure, and climate stress. However, agricultural landscapes are highly dynamic. A model trained in one region or growing season may fail when deployed in another geography, climate zone, year, or sensor condition.

This creates a fundamental out-of-distribution generalization problem in agricultural GeoAI. Models may achieve high accuracy under random train-test splits, but still learn unstable shortcuts related to geography, crop calendar, soil background, cloud artifacts, sensor conditions, or year-specific weather patterns. My work aims to address this gap by developing models that learn transferable agricultural signals rather than memorizing domain-specific biases.

Research Problem

How can we build multimodal GeoAI models that reliably monitor crop condition, agricultural stress, and production risk across unseen regions, years, climate regimes, and sensor conditions?

Key target applications include:

  • Crop type mapping
  • Early crop stress detection
  • Drought and flood impact monitoring
  • Irrigation and water-stress assessment
  • Yield-risk estimation
  • Food-security monitoring
  • Climate-resilient agricultural decision support

Methodological Direction: AgriShift

I am developing a framework tentatively called AgriShift, which explores selective domain residualization and reliability-aware multimodal fusion for agricultural remote sensing.

The core idea is to separate agricultural representations into three components:

  1. Stable crop and land structure: persistent field, crop, terrain, and land-cover characteristics.
  2. Domain nuisance information: unstable factors such as region-specific appearance, year-specific weather patterns, sensor artifacts, cloud effects, and label-source bias.
  3. Transferable crop or stress evidence: signals that should remain useful across new geographies, growing seasons, and climate conditions.

Instead of enforcing complete domain invariance, the goal is to learn selective invariance: suppress unstable nuisance factors while preserving useful environmental context such as phenology, weather, and climate information.

The framework also uses reliability-aware fusion to adaptively combine SAR, optical, weather, and static environmental features. Optical imagery captures vegetation spectral response and phenology but is sensitive to clouds and atmospheric effects. SAR provides complementary structure and moisture-related information under cloudy conditions but introduces sensor-specific noise and geometry effects. Weather and environmental variables provide critical climate context but often at coarser spatial resolution.

Experimental Focus

The first stage of this work investigates agricultural domain generalization using public Earth-observation datasets such as CropHarvest.

Initial deployment-style evaluation splits include:

  • Leave-country-out generalization
  • Leave-region-out generalization
  • Leave-year-out generalization
  • Leave-climate-zone-out generalization
  • Missing-modality or cloud-corruption stress tests

The goal is to compare standard ERM, SAR-only models, optical-only models, naive multimodal fusion, and domain-generalization baselines against AgriShift.

Beyond average accuracy, I evaluate:

  • Worst-domain performance
  • Out-of-distribution performance gap
  • Calibration and uncertainty
  • Domain leakage in learned representations
  • Modality reliability behavior
  • Robustness under missing or degraded sensor inputs

Broader Vision

The broader goal of this research is to develop robust, interpretable, and transferable GeoAI models for real-world agricultural and environmental monitoring. Rather than optimizing only for random benchmark splits, I aim to build systems that remain reliable in actual deployment conditions: new regions, future growing seasons, climate extremes, limited labels, and imperfect sensor observations.

This direction connects computer vision, remote sensing, domain generalization, multimodal learning, and climate-smart agriculture, with long-term impact for food security, water resilience, and climate adaptation.

In parallel, I continue work on robust UAV tracking and perception under sparse, noisy, and safety-critical sensing conditions, including spatiotemporal modeling, multimodal fusion, and reliability-aware inference.
